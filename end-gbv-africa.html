Understand

What gender-based violence looks like

GBV is harm rooted in unequal power between genders. It is never the survivor's fault, whatever form it takes.

Physical & sexual

Hitting, forced sex, FGM, or any unwanted physical act used to hurt or control.

Emotional & verbal

Threats, insults, humiliation, gaslighting, or constant criticism meant to break your confidence.

Economic & social

Controlling your money, blocking you from work or school, or isolating you from friends and family.

Digital abuse

Tracking your phone, sharing private images without consent, or online harassment and threats.

Harmful practices

Child or forced marriage, bride price abuse, and widow inheritance used against a woman's will.

Workplace & institutional

Sexual harassment, unequal treatment, or abuse of power by employers, teachers, or officials.

Plan ahead

Building a safety plan

A safety plan is a personal, practical guide for staying safer — whether you're still at home, preparing to leave, or already out.

01

Identify a safe word

Agree on a code word with a trusted friend or family member that signals you need help without saying so directly.

02

Pack a hidden go-bag

Keep copies of ID, some cash, a spare phone charger, and essential medication somewhere your abuser won't check.

03

Know two safe places

Identify a neighbour, relative, shelter, or place of worship you could go to quickly, day or night.

04

Save numbers discreetly

Store hotlines under a friend's name in your phone, or memorise them, in case your phone is checked.

05

Document incidents

If it's safe to do so, note dates, injuries, and messages — this can support a case later, but only if it doesn't put you at risk.

06

Talk to a counsellor

Hotline social workers can help you build a plan specific to your situation, for free and in confidence.

For friends & family

How to support a survivor

What you say and do in the first conversation matters. Here's a simple guide.

● Do

  • Listen without interrupting, and believe her.
  • Say clearly: "This is not your fault."
  • Let her make her own decisions about what happens next.
  • Offer to help her find a hotline, shelter, or counsellor.
  • Check in gently over time — healing isn't linear.

● Avoid

  • Asking "why didn't you just leave?"
  • Pressuring her to report or leave before she's ready.
  • Confronting the abuser without her consent — it can increase danger.
  • Sharing her story with others without permission.
  • Making the moment about your own shock or anger.
